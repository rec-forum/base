Session I: Today's kids are three times more likely to end up in hospital because they fell out of bed rather than out of a tree
http://www.dailymail.co.uk/news/article-3626687/Today-s-kids-likely-fall-bed-tree.html

* Expert claims children more likely to be hurt falling out of bed than a tree
* Dame Fiona Reynolds said children no longer have the room to run around
* Added children spend average six to seven hours a day in front of gadgets
* Said third of youngsters are overweight because they do not play outside

By REHEMA FIGUEIREDO FOR THE DAILY MAIL
PUBLISHED: 01:39 BST, 6 June 2016 | UPDATED: 03:10 BST, 6 June 2016

It's a far cry from the outdoor adventures of the Famous Five – or even the grittier exploits of the pupils of Grange Hill.
For, according to one academic, children are now three times more likely to end up in hospital because they have fallen out of bed rather than out of a tree.

Dame Fiona Reynolds, master of Emmanuel College at Cambridge University, believes parents in Britain need to rethink their children’s experiences as youngsters no longer have room to run around and play.

Speaking at the Hay Festival, she also warned that children who are currently growing up shielded from the harsher realities of the world – so-called ‘Generation Snowflake’ – have less space to run around than free range hens.

She said: ‘We have to think hard about the kind of society we’re creating for the future.

‘We think we’ve sorted out problems of deep poverty and deprivation but there’s something else we’re missing: access to nature.’

Dame Fiona, a former director general of the National Trust, said one of the most successful campaigns run by the organisation was ‘50 things to do before you are 11 and three quarters’ which offered parents ideas for taking their children outside.

She warned: ‘We’ve lost the area over which we let our children roam free by 90 per cent in a generation.

'Two thirds have less space to run around in than free range hens. A child today is three times more likely to go to hospital for falling out of bed than for falling out of a tree. This is about saying, “what kind of childhood experience do we want our children to have?”.’

Questions:
1) What is something you really miss from your childhood activities? Did you like climbing trees? Why very few kids are engaging in outdoor activities nowadays?
2) Children are more likely to be hurt falling out of bed than out of a tree. What do you think are the causes that result in this phenomenon?
3) Trying to recall your outdoor activities in your childhood, what did you remember? In retrospect, what today's children will remember about their childhood activities when they grow up?
4) To prevent future generation experiencing great risks than we have suffered, what our government and we have to do in a different way?

Session II: What does Hothouse Earth mean? Global warming, temperature rises and the events leading us to a tipping point
https://www.thesun.co.uk/tech/6958898/hothouse-earth-global-warming-temperature-rises-tipping-point/

POINT OF NO RETURN
What does Hothouse Earth mean? Global warming, temperature rises and the events leading us to a tipping point
Runaway 'domino effect' could make parts of the Earth uninhabitable even if we meet greenhouse gas targets, scientists warn

By Felix Allen
7th August 2018

HUMANITY faces catastrophe from runaway global warming even if we meet targets to cut greenhouse gas emissions, scientists have warned.
Ten tipping point dangers could spark a domino-effect cascade of disaster, creating irreversible "Hothouse Earth" conditions. Here's what we know.

What is Hothouse Earth?

A group of scientists have delivered a grim warning in a paper published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences.
It spells out how natural processes that normally protect us from the effects of climate change could turn against us and make the problem far worse.

Forests, oceans and soil permafrost have absorbed huge quantities of carbon dioxide and methane, a more potent greenhouse gas.

But they could release billions of tons of the gases if global average temperatures rise to just 2C above pre-industrial levels - the maximum in the 2015 Paris Agreement signed by 196 nations.

Linked processes would generate a "positive feedback" loop, amplifying and accelerating the effects as the earth warms by 5C or even more.
The Amazon rainforest is a huge carbon sink but could start pumping out greenhouse gases as it dies back in a warmer climate
The study warns: "The Earth System may be approaching a planetary threshold that could lock in a continuing, rapid pathway towards much hotter conditions - Hothouse Earth."

Beyond this tipping point, any actions by humans to reduce global warming would be futile.

Temperatures would rise to higher than at any time in the past 1.2million years, they say.

Seas could rise more than 80ft and deadly heatwaves, droughts, famines and superstorms would make much of the planet uninhabitable.

What are the ten factors that lead to Hothouse Earth?

The study identifies ten climate change processes that could combine to bring about a global catastrophe.
Temperature rises of between 1C and 5C would trip these natural processes "from friend to foe".

They are:
 * reduced northern hemisphere snow cover
 * loss of Arctic summer sea ice
 * reduced Antarctic sea ice
 * melting polar ice sheets
 * thawing permafrost
 * the release of methane trapped on the ocean floor
 * weakening land and ocean carbon sinks
 * increased carbon dioxide production by ocean bacteria
 * Amazon rainforest die-back
 * coniferous forest die-back

"These tipping elements can potentially act like a row of dominoes," said co-author Prof Johan Rockström of the Stockholm Resilience Centre in Sweden.

For instance, less snow and ice would mean more of the sun's rays are absorbed (because ice is like a mirror that bounces heat back into space).

The extent of Arctic sea ice has dropped by 40 per cent in four decades and could vanish altogether one year in four, scientists warn.

Rising temperatures would weaken "carbon sinks" in the land and seas that currently absorb around half of carbon pollution.

Forests are already shrinking and oceans are thought to be close to the point where they cannot absorb any more CO2.

Warming of 3C - only around 2C higher than at present - would kill around 40 per cent of the Amazon rainforest, releasing billions of tons of carbon.

The vast forests in Canada increased their biomas for much of the 20th century - locking in carbon - but have been declining since the 1970s thanks to climate-linked fires and insect swarms.

Permafrost in the vast frozen plains of Russia and Canada contain CO2 and methane equivalent to 15 years of human emissions.

It could be released as the ground thaws, accelerating warming further.

Similarly, crystals of methane hydrates in the ocean floor are thought to be vulnerable to global warming.

Scientists think they have been responsible for periods of rapid warming in geological history, leading to mass extinctions.

More severe warming would eventually melt the massive polar ice caps in Greenland and the Antarctic, raising sea levels by up 25m.
This would swamp most of the world's major cities and the agricultural land that feeds them.

Scientists have said in this scenario the planet would be able to sustain no more than a billion people, down from more than seven billion now.

Can anything be done to stop it?

In 2015 governments of the world committed to keeping temperature rises below 2C of warming above pre-industrial levels, and to strive to keep below 1.5C.

Even that might not be enough if the analysis by the team of experts is correct.

Prof Johan Rockström told the BBC: "What we are saying is that when we reach 2C of warming, we may be at a point where we hand over the control mechanism to Planet Earth herself.

"We are the ones in control right now, but once we go past 2C, we see that the Earth system tips over from being a friend to a foe."

Questions:
1) According to the articles, what does “Hothouse Earth” mean? And what are the factors that lead to it.
2) What is the grim warning that scientists deliver in this article?
How can natural processes protect us from the effect of climate change? If the natural processes no longer protect us, will that amplify the problems?
3) Why is the Amazon rainforest not absorbing as much greenhouse gases as in the past? How does this lead to Global warming?
4) What’s your feeling when you read this: Seas could rise more than 80ft and deadly heatwaves, droughts, famines and superstorms would make much of the planet uninhabitable.
5) Are you anxious about Taiwan’s shrinking forests being gradually replaced by high buildings? How can we do to balance between social development and Global warming?
6) Rapid warming can lead to rising sea levels & mass extinctions. As individual, what can we do to slow the effects of Global warming?
